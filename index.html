INTRODUCTION

First impressions are everything. This is true in almost every aspect of life, however this is perhaps no more true than in the tech industry where companies live and die by aesthetics. In the eyes of the end-user, a buggy UI or an error prone app conveys a deadly degree of unprofessionalism. There is nothing more likely to kill a business relationship and send customers on their merry way for all eternity than a user experience fraught with frustration.

A frustrating user experience can occur in many ways. One of the more common ways is via Responsive Design, or how your UI transitions between various screen sizes. Big screen monitors, laptops, tablets and smart phones, etc, all require different viewing configurations. Designing a website to accommodate an array of devices can get very complicated, very quickly. However complicated from an engineering perspective, your UI must be the furthest thing from complicated to the user -- unless the mission is to receive from competitors as many thank you cards as possible. In all seriousness, there is nothing that says to the customer that I care about you and your business more than a well-polished product that works like magic. Inspire needs to do just that: inspire!

Bug 001

Navigation Toggle

Looking over Inspire’s Dashboard interface, its apparent that considerable thought has been put into the overall idea and business model. And while the high level conceptualization is out of this world cool, the implementation is less than stellar in many places, and even sloppy in others. In less than five minutes of looking I found several pain points.

Problem

Navigation Menu

One of the things I noticed first about Inspire’s website was the Dashboard navigational menu, located on the left-hand side of the layout. Initially, the overall design looked quite good.

On closer inspection a few serious functionality problems stood out. For starters, having a laptop with a smaller screen resolution, I noticed that the behavior of the menu’s toggle button was affected by the browser’s window height. By adjusting the vertical window size larger or smaller, the button would actually traverse up and down the y-axis, at times covering other buttons and making them inaccessible. This effect can be seen below.

Here we can see that if a user reduces the browser window height, the UI functionality becomes partly unusable. This makes it impossible to say, look at two stocks at one time, or cross-reference secondary information.

Expectation

Two Problems

As we can see above, the Dashboard UI fails one of the most basic principles of Responsive Design: resizability. While technically more of a design flaw then a full fledged bug, this issue boils down to two elementary errors. The first error was the failure of the developer to anticipate the potential problem of positioning stationary buttons above non-stationary buttons capable of traversing the y-axis per window height. The second error was allowing the menu to scroll in the first place, which makes very little sense with only six icons. The expectation, should be to have the icons positioned as follows.

Notice when the browser is at full window height, the menu bug is hidden. Now lets look at the next screen.

Bug 002

Text Cut Off

In this next section, we will be looking at another area of the Dashboard that has a few opportunities for improvement. Before we get into the nuts and bolts of the problem, lets take a look at the following.

As you can see above, something has clearly gone awry--the last line of the text is completely cut off! While the overall component does have nice aesthetics, since we are being honest here, the text cut off at the bottom does not paint an appearance of professionalism. Before we make any changes though, lets run the gamut of screen sizes and get a full scope of the potential problem.

Less Than 600px

Here we can see a that screen size less than 600px appears to have no obvious issues. In other words, if you are surfing the Dashboard on your smartphone or some mid-range sized tablet, you should have no issue whatsoever.

At mid-range resolutions, such as tablets and small computers, the text on the bottom line is cut short.

Now that we have isolated the problem, the next step is to fix it. In order to do this however, we must first analyze what is happening on a deeper level.

Solution

Modify CSS

Looking under the hood, it is easy to determine that the issue at hand is rooted in the developer’s approach to handling the dynamic resizing of the x-axis when transitioning between different screen sizes. With modern development methodologies, such as test driven development, CICD, Agile processes, etc, it is very rare that such issues would not be identified before being pushed into production level code. This is especially true considering how simple it would be to suss out a value that would work for all three targeted screen ranges.

This is a simple fix, to be quite frank. All that is required is plugging in a couple different values into the code highlighted in red below, and seeing which work across all three targeted screen resolutions.

.
news-small__blurb
[
_ngcontent-veh-c15
]{

padding
:0.5em 0;

max-height:49%;

overflow
:hidden;
text-overflow
:ellipsis;
}

Playing with the values a bit, it was quick and easy to determine that a value of 54% for the max-height property would do the trick. This change is highlighted in green below.

.
news-small__blurb
[
_ngcontent-veh-c15
]{

padding
:0.5em 0;

max-height:54%;

overflow
:hidden;
text-overflow
:ellipsis;
}

As we can see by comparing the elements below, the first of which does not have the fix applied, and the second of which does, the issue has been completely resolved in the latter. Although not shown here, this fix is fluid across all targeted screen resolutions.

Bug 003

Preview

While the first two fixes were more lightweight in nature, an important future optimization could be correcting a display bug that seems to occur on the visualization interface of a given stock’s performance history. Although further analysis is necessary, it appears that a bug occurs specifically when the line transitions from red to green, or green to red, as highlighted below with the two circles. As you can see, within these circles, strange looking horizontal lines exude from the transition point and make the visualization unrepresentative of the data it displays in certain places.

Considering the visualization of a given stock performance history is a very prominent part of the user experience, this should be corrected as soon as possible.
